Financial Impacts of Dark Data

It's well known that storing data has a cost. While storing photos, videos, and applications on personal devices may seem small, it adds up. For businesses, these costs are far more significant. Omdia estimates that annual data storage costs hit around $57 billion, with forecasts predicting an increase to $128 billion by 2028. If half of this data is dark data, it's crucial to reduce and optimize dark data storage.

In Context: Netflix Spending on Data Storage

Netflix collects around $8 billion in quarterly revenue, with growth year over year. The company relies on AWS storage servers, paying approximately $28 million monthly—or about $366 million annually—for media storage. Although manageable for Netflix, it raises questions: how much of this data is essential for service functionality?

Industry Expert Prukalpa Sankar estimates that 65% of Netflix's stored data is dark data, which equates to around $6.2 million monthly on largely untouched data. This cost could be better allocated if time and resources were dedicated to dark data reduction and management.

Another Financial Impact of Dark Data: Breach Risk

With data storage comes breach risks, especially with dark data. Improperly stored data can lead to costly legal repercussions and fines.

The Equifax Data Breach

Equifax, a credit reporting company, faced the devastating impact of dark data mismanagement in September 2017, when a massive data breach exposed personal information of nearly 150 million Americans, including sensitive details like credit scores, full names, and addresses. Improper management of dark data worsened the situation, exposing even more private data.

Equifax faced multiple state fines and a settlement costing around $1.3 billion. The breach left Equifax with financial loss and a damaged reputation.
