The Positive Uses of Dark Data

Whilst data scientists and economists have justified concerns about dark data, it is important to understand that, like all things, dark data can be used in positive ways.

Organizing Dark Data

The hard part, as with all things, is organizing the data. For many organizations, dark data has been piling up for decades, and going through it all is a long, monotonous task that requires a huge amount of manpower. As we enter the AI era, this task will become far easier, and more companies may start to clear out their storage.

Productive Uses

Once all the data has been prepared, it can be used productively to optimize the systems used by businesses daily. Dark data's potential is untapped, but some of the few things it could be used for include: improving supply chain systems, enhancing customer satisfaction, increasing a business's general productivity, and training AI.

Training AI for Specialized Tasks

AI learns like a child; it grows its knowledge by reading information and experiencing things. Nothing but the very basics was ever programmed into an AI system. Dark data can be used to expedite the time it takes for AI to learn a specific skill. Essentially, more data means a greater bank of knowledge, allowing the AI to be more accurate in its responses.

Optimizing Manufacturing and Supply Chain Systems

One of the most significant causes of delays in manufacturing is the lack of consistency or poor configuration in machinery. An article by Splunk on the topic mentioned that by analyzing automatically generated system logs, systems can be tweaked and recalibrated to ensure the best possible performance, leading to more time-efficient systems and ultimately better profit margins.

Improving General Customer Experience

For the success of a business, it is vital that the customer has a good experience with the business and the products or services that it offers. From analyzing dark data, patterns of behavior can usually be found, such as the popularity of a product in certain areas, the time between purchases, or the success of marketing strategies. This information can be put together to form a larger picture, allowing businesses to tailor their strategies to attract more sales.

Social Media Engagement

A largely untapped market for easy brand analysis is social media. Social media interactions and analytics qualify as dark data, per the definition. Today, social media is one of the most effective and simplest ways to understand public sentiment about a product. Almost every social media platform features engagement metrics (e.g., watch time, views, likes, etc.) that are widely used by companies.

The other component of dark data from social media, the physical engagement, is far less used but just as indicative. Monitoring customer messages and comments—asking questions or providing feedback—are rarely practiced, but they are an easy way to enhance the understanding of public perception regarding the brand. In an article on the topic, Alexis C. Madrigal of The Atlantic commented: “Surprisingly, almost 70% of all social media interactions with brands are classified as dark data.”
